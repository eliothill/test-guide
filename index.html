Proposition - what we do

Citizens Advice currently offers advice digitally across a vast range of topics. We try to cover every angle, despite there being little or no evidence that we need to do so.

As a charity competing in a saturated marketplace, we now need to be ruthless in focusing on the core proposition of the organisation: we help people to solve a problem.

We don’t need to tell people what something is - they wouldn’t be coming to us if they didn’t know it affects them - we need to tell them what they can do with their current circumstance.

To give a strong focus to our digital offering we need to split our work into the 2 things people need (not want) from us:

  • problem resolution
  • clear information about rights and entitlements

Whatever channel we use (face to face, web chat, self-service) needs to give the user (the adviser helping a client or the client themselves) the knowledge to make an informed decision about their next steps.

All content must be actionable - even if the action is ‘I know what my options are, I now need to choose one that will help me with the situation I have come for advice for’.

Audiences

We have 2 audiences: clients and advisers. They will approach the problem in different ways - but the problem is the same. As content designers we need to make the person with the problem feel they own the problem - and the solution. Because then they can do something about it and actually control their situation.

Our services - on whatever channel - need to be:

  • accessible, usable and relevant to anyone
  • non-judgmental - we must trust what the client tells us and support them without questioning their integrity

Throughout all our content and services, we don’t look at people in terms of their race/gender/medical condition etc. We are simply dealing with people with a problem. But we must provide content, tools and services that always recognise people may have specific concerns (like a disability). In short, we must always provide inclusive advice.

We will be respectful that problems often don't come in isolation and will design solutions around common groups of evidenced problems and needs.

We create content, tools and services that clients can understand and use themselves. Equally, advisers can use our services to help clients in a way that means the clients still own their problem - and the solution that we, as an organisation, advise them to pursue.

Online clients

Our largest audience is online - people looking for advice to enable them to resolve their problems.

We develop services and content that meet their evidenced needs so they can find a way forward using content and services that:

  • give them appropriate, responsible and actionable advice at the point of need
  • clarify their rights and responsibilities so they can make an informed decision about their next steps

Where the client doesn’t know what the root of their problem is, we will help them discover it as the first step towards resolution.

We provide content and services (highly optimised for search) that offer preventative advice, as well as cure.

We don't tell people about 'things' - we will focus on giving the best advice to enable them to make the necessary actions that will solve their problem. While we will provide context, we won't solely explain ‘things' - unless we're the only organisation that can.

For example:

becomes

Advisers

Our hypothesis is that if we serve clients with advice that shows them how to resolve their problem or understand their rights, that will allow advisers to give the best advice in the majority of cases.

But we must recognise there will be complex or unusual cases where advisers will need an authoritative source of detail to infer the best course of action for their client.

Following the model being developed in Watson, we will link to the most authoritative and trusted sources of detail (legislative or practical). Where no authoritative and trusted sources exist, we will create and maintain content and services to meet these evidenced needs.

Subject areas that we cover in content and digital services

We cover areas that clients view as strengths of Citizens Advice and expect to get the best advice from the organisation. When we cover an area, we cover it holistically for clients, advisers and clients with advisers.

Core areas are:

  • money - budgeting and income maximisation, dealing with debt, managing creditors, debt remedies, bailiffs
  • benefits - entitlements, how to claim, problems with claims, ongoing management of benefits
  • goods and services: your rights - rights and redress when buying, renting or leasing goods or services, managing utility suppliers, identifying unfair trading practices and resolving disputes
  • housing - problems with landlords, tenancy agreements, mortgages and conveyancing, disputes with neighbours, eviction and homelessness
  • relationships - legal rights in relationships, ending relationships, safeguarding children at the end of relationships
  • immigration and nationality - rights for non-UK nationals living in the UK including employment, benefits, access to family, housing and asylum (to the appropriate regulated level)
  • dealing with court cases - conciliation and mediation in disputes, bringing a civil claim, defending a civil claim, being a witness at a criminal trial
  • workplace rights - contractual entitlements, legal rights as a worker or employee, including pay and time off
  • workplace problems - discipline and grievance, dismissal, redundancy, disputes, conciliation, employment tribunals
  • safeguarding vulnerable people - dealing with child abuse, social care, getting support for people with mental health issues
  • dealing with discrimination - identifying discrimination, challenging discrimination, dispute resolution
  • access to education - getting access to education, resolving problems with schools

Statutory responsibilities

We support the organisation’s statutory responsibilities to provide advice, guidance and education in the areas of consumer rights, energy supply and post.

Funded activities and services

Citizens Advice often gets funding to deliver a particular service (for example, Pension Wise). The digital team supports these services by providing content that matches the service to a user need. For example we tell users when and how to access the Pension Wise service within our existing pensions content, where relevant.

Digital principles

These principles are a benchmark for everything the digital team does, and the way we work. They also guide what’s in and what’s out of scope.

They’re both aspirational and achievable. We might not get them perfectly right every time, but they’re what we should be striving towards - no matter what we’re working on.

The principles interact with each other at all stages of our work.

1. Start with people’s needs

  • everything we do is based on people’s needs
  • needs come from real people - online users, clients and advisers, not the organisation or policy
  • people’s needs are based on problems, eg ‘as someone who suspects they’ve been trafficked, I need to get help so I can get out of my situation’
  • needs are collected through research and data
  • we don’t assume anything - the digital team isn’t the audience, and doesn’t try to be

2. Design with data and research

  • every decision should be informed by research, eg content, CMS development, design, usability
  • use resources like Google Analytics, Google Trends, SEMrush, Petra - these are your ports of call when making decisions
  • do as much research as possible - with real people who’d be using whatever you’re working on
  • try not to assume - question everything
  • only the people who use our products know best
  • we iterate with data and research too (it’s not just a ‘big bang’ launch)

3. Help people solve problems

  • identify problems through research and solve them with advice
  • don’t just give information or explain the law without solving problems
  • make content active and actionable
  • we define how we meet needs with ‘acceptance criteria’ - a set list of criteria which explain what it takes for us to solve people’s problems
  • make our solutions easy to find, externally and internally

4. Collaborate with the whole Citizens Advice service

  • within the digital team: content designers, designers, researchers, developers, delivery managers, data scientists
  • but also outside the team: policy experts, SMEs, campaigners, news team and advisers
  • use Slack to get the team’s opinion on a problem, no matter how small
  • everyone can contribute to the conversation
  • don’t struggle alone - there’s a group of smart and humble people around you, ready and willing to pitch in (and you can return the favour one day)
  • understand the importance of the role that we have in the service

5. Give practical and tactical advice

  • this is where we add value
  • give the online audience the advice that no one else is saying
  • we find the best tactics by learning from advisers, subject matter experts and by doing our own research
  • we can really push the boundaries - and replicate what CAB has been doing for years in a different space

6. Understand the different audiences

  • know online audience needs and adviser needs
  • sometimes you can meet the needs of both at once
  • sometimes you’ll have to design only for advisers, or only for the public
  • know where this line is, and when you have to write separate pieces
  • this is achieved through research
  • different subject areas have different audiences

7. Use one voice

  • use a consistent tone of voice and style across the organisation
  • apply the the same consistency to tools, the branding and design
  • the style guide and 2i people are the guides for this
  • be consistent not uniform - there will be exceptions to the rules but they need to be discussed and justified, preferably with research
  • test the style regularly
  • think about accessibility not as a bolt-on, but as an integral part of the design process

8. Share early, fail fast and then improve

  • don’t spend too long on something without a second opinion or a crit - don’t strive for perfection
  • do small pieces of work that can be iterated
  • you’ll always benefit from a fresh pair of eyes (and regularly)
  • build quick prototypes of tools that can be tested as soon as possible
  • test your content and the language you’re using with your audience as soon as you can
  • aim for a minimum viable product, then fill the gaps
  • failure is not a weakness - it’s part of the process

9. Keep making it better

  • be prepared to release products and publish content that aren’t perfect, because digital is never finished
  • content and products will never be perfect, but we make the best decisions we can with the data we have at the time
  • monitor your live content and iterate based on data
  • it’s our job to keep learning and never stop

Formats[a]

Short form

Content that is clear and concise, aimed a specific user with a single issue.

Examples include: beta content page

Long form

Users like to have as much of the content related to one area on a single page, but they also only want to see what’s relevant to them.

When we need to show variations of a single caveat, or consecutive stages of a process, we use:

- accordions

We need to allow users to easily access particular areas of content on the page as soon as they need to – and to be as frictionless as possible so they can find the answers they need. For this we use:

- content navigation

Tools

Finding an answer shouldn't have to mean searching or reading through pages of content or understanding complex caveats. Where we need to simplify a route through a process or help someone to make a decision, we build tools that meet the needs of the user.

We build tools that are clear and easy to use - tools that and simplify the process of understanding eligibility, expectations or actions.

Process:

  • discover what the problem is
  • decide what the user need is
  • consider the value of a tool, and why flat content is inappropriate to meet the user need

Consider the context of the tool: is it an embedded quick checker that tells the user if the content on the page is relevant to them? Or is it a more complex decision tree that has multiple possible outcomes and will link out depending on answers?

Tools include:

  • checkers - a linear path with binary output of yes or no
  • decision trees - variable path with advised output
  • template letter generator
  • printable prompt sheets (‘preparing for your Universal Credit interview’)
  • interactive document (‘understanding your energy bill’)

These tools are built with the users in mind, so that they can complete tasks independently. But they are also for use by advisers and volunteers who help clients resolve issues in bureaux and outreach locations.

Digital tone principles

These principles work together to create the tone of the content.

Concise

Our content should give users only what they need to solve their problems, without waffle or repetition. Be brief and clear, but comprehensive - focus on actions that client needs to take to solve their problem.

Content that was stripped of full details was deemed to be ‘too short’ in testing - so be sure to elaborate and contextualise where necessary. Examples and timescales tested very well.

Conversational

This may seem to be in conflict with writing concisely, but our research shows that conversational language:

  • is expected from Citizens Advice
  • is more trusted than a dry, purely factual tone

Aim for a balance. Conversational language can be slightly harder to understand compared to dry, factual content - so be wary of overdoing it.

Don’t let conversational language get in the way of clarity or getting to the point - having a conversational tone doesn’t mean adding in unnecessary information. Avoid colloquial devices like idioms and metaphors - they will get in the way of users’ understanding of the content.

Supportive

We need to be supportive for users who might feel like they’re taking on the world alone. This means:

  • unpicking complicated processes so they seem less daunting
  • guiding them through processes in a way they understand
  • explaining their rights in a way that is clear and actionable

Our research showed that users felt they were more likely to follow instructions if they were written in a supportive tone.

Content design style

Style points

Abbreviations and acronyms

[h]

 

The first time you use an abbreviation or acronym on a page, write it out in full, followed by the short version in brackets.

Example: “Send the form to Her Majesty’s Revenue and Customs (HMRC).”

 

Use the acronym from then on.

Don’t use an acronym if you only use the term once on the page, unless it’s needed for clarity (eg the acronym is more well known than the full term).

 

Don’t make up acronyms, only use them where they’re known, eg say ‘Universal Credit’, not ‘UC’.

Don’t write out well known acronyms, eg NHS, UK, VAT, GCSE.

Don’t use full stops in acronyms, including in “eg” and “ie”.

Active voice

Use active voice, not passive voice. Front-load sentences with actions, and address the user.

Active: “Fill in the form.”

Passive: “The form must be filled in.”

Addressing the user

 

Use 'you' when writing for clients.

Ampersands

Don’t use “&”.

Always use “and” unless it’s part of a brand logo that you need to write in text, eg M&S.

Don’t use ampersands in government department names, eg use “HM Revenue and Customs”, “Department for Work and Pensions”.

[i]

Bold 

 

Bold makes phrases and keywords stand out, so it should be used only when you want to emphasise text or help users find and return to a key term.

Be careful when using bold - clients might be drawn to what you put in bold and miss something important because it’s not in bold.

Bullets

Bullets help to make web content readable. Make sure that:

  • you always use a lead-in line
  • the lead-in line has a colon, not a dash or “:-”
  • each bullet makes sense running on from the lead-in line
  • you use lower case at the start of each bullet
  • you don’t have more than one sentence in a bullet point - use a dash, comma or brackets to split ideas up (this is challenging)
  • you don’t put commas or semicolons after bullet points - not even the last one (see below for use of full stops)
  • the bullet points are short - any more than 2 lines and you should think about whether the format is correct

You can use the lead-in line to tell the user how many of the the bullets they need to act on. Sometimes it’s obvious but sometimes it needs explaining.

Examples:

“Include all of the following in your complaint letter:”

“You can do one of the following:”

Try not to use multiple bullet lists that are close together - this is confusing for users. An exception is if you have a list that is long that needs breaking up (consider breaking it up if there’s more than 10 bullets).

See ‘steps’ for numbered lists.

Capitalisation 

Using familiar forms and word shapes helps with word recognition - so do some research and use capitals if they are commonly used (eg in official sources, forums or other policy sources). Lower case is generally prefered if there is ambiguity.

“The Guardian style guide says the following about capitals: 'A look through newspaper archives would show greater use of capitals the further back you went. The tendency towards lowercase, which in part reflects a less formal, less deferential society, has been accelerated by the explosion of the internet: some web companies, and many email users, have dispensed with capitals altogether.'

Use capitals for:

  • government departments (eg, HM Revenue and Customs)
  • proper nouns, eg organisation names and brand names
  • titles of specific acts or bills, eg Housing Reform Bill (but use ‘the act’ or ‘the bill’ after the first time you use the full act or bill title)
  • header cells in tables, eg Annual profits
  • titles of publications
  • religions, eg Jewish, Muslim

Don’t capitalise:

  • government – never Government, even when referring to an elected administration
  • sections or schedules within specific named acts, regulations or orders
  • policy themes, eg sustainable communities, promoting economic growth, local enterprise zones
  • seasons: summer, autumn, winter, spring

Check the A-Z guide to see if capitals are used, or use the ‘Oxford Dictionary for Writers and Editors’, or the Guardian style guide.

Citizens Advice - referring to ourselves

Citizens Advice as an organisation is singular, ie ‘Citizens Advice has done research’ (not ‘have done research’).

The recent rebrand discourages the use of ‘bureaux’ - instead, say “nearest Citizens Advice”.

Commas

 

Don’t use the ‘oxford comma’ (final comma in a list) to separate the last 2 items in a list unless you need it to remove ambiguity or confusion. For example:

“You don’t need to pay the financial requirement if you’re getting JSA, ESA or Universal Credit.”

Also, consider using bullets for comma-separated lists.

Contractions 

 

Use contractions where possible, eg ‘you’ll’, ‘can’t’ and ‘don’t’.

Don’t use ‘should’ve’, ‘could’ve’ or ‘would’ve’ - these are hard to read.

Dates and times

When using dates:

  • the general style is “24 December 2014”
  • when space is an issue, eg tables, publication titles etc, you can use truncated months, eg Jan, Feb, Mar - but only where absolutely necessary (screen readers will not pick this up well)
  • use ‘to’ in date ranges – not hyphens. Eg “January to March”
  • use ‘midnight’ and ‘midday’, not ‘12pm’ or ‘12am’
  • don’t use ‘quarter’ or ‘Q1’ for reporting/accounting periods - use the months, for example: ‘Expenses, Jan to Mar 2013’
  • tax years are written as “tax year 2012 to 2013”. For multiple tax years, use common sense to make it clear. This is good: “The rate was £3 a week for the 3 tax years between 6 April 2008 and 5 April 2012.”

Domestic violence and abuse

Use the following interchangeably when writing for an audience that is experiencing or has experienced domestic violence or abuse:

  • ‘living with domestic abuse or violence’
  • ‘a victim of domestic abuse or violence’
  • ‘have experienced domestic abuse or violence’
  • ‘experiencing domestic abuse or violence’

Important points:

  • these clients are identifying their experience as ‘abuse’
  • the experts we spoke to used ‘violence’ rather than ‘domestic violence’ (but we also need use language that ensures users can find our content, hence why we use ‘domestic abuse’)

Drugs, alcohol and gambling

Use ‘problem’ or ‘problems with’ when writing about drugs/alcohol/gambling.

Don’t use ‘abuse’ when talking about these topics.

Examples

“You can ask for more frequent payments of your benefits if you have a drug or alcohol problem.”

“You can ask for the Jobcentre to pay your rent directly to your landlord if you have problems with drugs or alcohol.”

Examples

Our research has shown that examples are very popular with clients. Use examples to tell stories that illustrate situations or calculations.

Forms

Users “fill in” forms, not “fill out” forms.

Headings and titles

Headings are used to scan the content in order to pick out what parts to read in detail.

Headings and titles must:

  • be active where possible, eg “Get help to pay for healthcare”

  • include keywords for search engine optimisation

  • have colons where necessary, not dashes

  • be in sentence case with no full stop

  • not have acronyms, unless they’re well known

  • not be a question
  • address the user as “you”, not “I”

Bad: “How much Universal Credit will I get?”

Good: “How much Universal Credit you’ll get”

If a subheading is for a particular audience use “If” to define that audience, ie:

“If you’ve already applied for Universal Credit”

not: “You’ve already applied for Universal Credit”

Don’t put jargon or technical terms in headings. People are drawn to headings, and they become immediately confused because they don’t understand the jargon. Use language that the user can relate to.

[j]

Instructions

The following instructions were tested, and are listed from most urgent to least urgent:

  • You must
  • Do [this activity] as soon as possible
  • It’s important that you
  • You have to
  • You need to
  • You should
  • Contact [this person]
  • Write a letter
  • It’s a good idea to
  • It helps if you

Italics 

 

Don’t use italics. Publication titles must be in single quotation marks and in title case.

Jargon

 

Avoid jargon wherever possible. Plain English should be used at all times. It’s sometimes necessary to use jargon so that clients are aware of the processes they’re going through (eg ‘mandatory reconsideration’) or their rights (eg ‘statute-barred debt’).

When these terms are first introduced on a page, you need to give a simple explanation, then put the term in single quotations.

Example:

“You can ask the Jobcentre to look at new evidence and review their decision - this is called ‘mandatory reconsideration’.”

You can then refer to the term without single quotations.

Don’t assume that the user has read and understood the term on another page.

Links and websites

Link in-line in normal paragraph text with useful phrases.

Don’t use a URL as link text, unless writing out contact details (using the style for contacts), in this case the correct format is www.website.com - with “www” but not with “https://”.

Be aware of user journeys and inline links - you’re potentially leading people away from the page at that point. They might not come back. How do you make sure they read what they need to? There’s no silver bullet, but something to keep in mind.

When linking to an active step in a user journey, make sure the link text is active:

Example:

“Some investments have more risk than others. You should get help from a financial adviser before deciding on how you invest your money.”

When linking to descriptive content, contextualise the link rather than just saying “read more” or “find out more” (give context in the link text if you use these):

        

Bad example:

“There are different tax rates for each pension option. Read more.”

        Better example:

        “Read about different tax rates for each pension option.”

Make sure you let users know if they’ll be taken away from our site:

Example:

“The Pensions Advisory Service has a pension comparison tool that can help you decide what pension is best for you.”

Avoid giving long descriptions of external links:

        Bad example:

“The Money Advice Service is a free, independent service. Their website has lots of useful information about pensions including comparison tables for choosing a personal pension provider and a pension calculator for working out how much pension you'll need. There's also a range of leaflets to help answer your pensions and retirement questions. Go to: www.moneyadviceservice.org.uk.”

Better example:

“You can get free and independent advice on pensions from the Money Advice Service.”

Content is “on” a website, not “at” a website. For example:

“Use the budgeting tool on the Money Advice Service website.”

 

When to use external links

Our research showed that clients often want to read more than one source when solving their problems, so it can be valuable to link out to related content and other forms of support. But be sure the link adds something that we’re not offering (rather than linking to very similar content). Calculators and tools are especially useful.

 

Don’t link to commercial sites unless absolutely necessary (eg in the rare case they’re providing a vital service that’s not found anywhere else). If you do, run it past an SME and try to include more than one for impartiality.

New window vs same window

Internal links (links to other pages on our sites) are always in the same window.

In general, external links (links to other sites) should also be in the same window. However, you might have good reason for using a new window. For example, if you’re giving guidance on how to fill in an online form - the user might want to switch between our advice and the online form. Therefore it’s a good idea to use a new window. Do research and user test this wherever possible.

Measurements 

Don’t use a space in abbreviated measurements - it should be 3,500kg, not 3,500 kg.

Meta descriptions

Meta descriptions appear underneath the page title in search results. Users will skim these (at best) so they need to be loaded with keywords and kept short and list-like. Over 2 lines of copy here should be a red flag. Put a full stop at the end.

Example:
Apply for Universal Credit
How to apply online, by phone or in person, what information you’ll need, where to get help and what happens next.

Numbers 

When using numbers:

  • use digits, even for numbers under 10, eg “send 2 copies of the bank statement” 
  • be careful of using decimals that are confusing, eg “54.5 years old” should be “54 years and 6 months old” 
  • use commas in thousands: 1,000, 10,000, 110,000
  • use “m” and decimals for millions: 1m, 1.5m, 20m
  • use “bn” and decimals for billions : 1bn, 1.5bn, 20bn
  • use “%” symbol for percentages
[k]

 Use of ‘1’ or ‘one’ in running text

Use 'one' (written out) unless you’re referring to a numbered step, point in a list or other situation where the numeral makes more sense to use.

Only use the numeral where it's something like “in point 1 of the design patterns", or where you’re referring to digits in the same sentence or list.

 

Publication titles

Publication titles must be in single quotation marks and in title case. No italics.

Quotation marks

Use single quotation marks:

  • when describing or explaining jargon for the first time, eg: “You can ask the Jobcentre to look at new evidence and review their decision - this is called ‘mandatory reconsideration’.”
  • for publication titles

Semicolons

Generally unnecessary. Do not use.

Steps

Use numbered steps to explain a process. Each step needs to be a full sentence. A lead in line is not necessary.

 

Telephone numbers

 

Use spaces between city and local exchange etc. Here are the different formats you should use:

 

01273 800 900

020 7450 4000

0800 890 567

07771 900 900

+44 (0)20 7450 4000

 

Time

 

Use 2pm or 11:30am - note colons and no spaces

Use ‘to’ when giving a range of times. For example, “from 2pm to 5pm’. Don’t use dashes.

The correct format is ‘6 hours 30 minutes’ rather than ‘6.5 hours’ or ‘6 and a half hours’. It’s the least ambiguous and requires the least calculation.
